# Pitch

You are crafting a pitch. Not a feature list — a STORY that makes the audience care about a problem and believe you're the one to solve it. Whether it's for investors, stakeholders, or internal leadership, a great pitch follows the same structure: make them feel the pain, show them the cure, prove you can deliver it.

**Topic:** $ARGUMENTS

---

## The Pitch Structure

Build the pitch in this order. Every slide/section should make the audience want to see the next one.

### 1. The Hook (1 slide / 30 seconds)

Open with something that makes the audience sit up:

- A surprising statistic about the problem
- A relatable scenario that triggers recognition
- A bold claim about the opportunity

**Not:** "We're building a platform that leverages AI to optimize..." (nobody cares yet)
**Instead:** "Tunisian consumers spend 780-1,560 TND per year on habitual purchases and get zero back. That's a $31M problem." (now they're listening)

### 2. The Problem (1-2 slides)

Make the audience FEEL the problem before you offer a solution.

- **Who** has this problem? (Be specific — not "businesses" but "small café owners in Tunisia with 50-200 customers/day")
- **What** is the pain? (Concrete scenarios, not abstract statements)
- **How bad** is it? (Numbers: money lost, time wasted, opportunity cost)
- **Why** hasn't it been solved? (What's broken about current solutions?)

**Test:** After this section, the audience should be thinking "somebody should fix this." If they're not, the problem isn't clear enough.

### 3. The Solution (1-2 slides)

NOW show the solution — and only now.

- **What** you do, in ONE sentence. (If it takes a paragraph, it's too complicated)
- **How** it works — the simplest possible explanation. (Demo, screenshot, or 3-step flow)
- **Before/after.** Show the user's world without your solution vs with it.

**Test:** Can a smart person who's never seen this explain it to someone else in 30 seconds? If not, simplify.

### 4. The "Why Now" (1 slide)

Why is this the right moment for this solution?

- Market shift (regulation change, technology breakthrough, cultural shift)
- Enabling technology (something is now possible/affordable that wasn't before)
- Growing pain point (the problem is getting worse, not better)
- Timing relative to competition (window of opportunity)

### 5. Market Size (1 slide)

Prove the opportunity is big enough to matter.

```
TAM (Total Addressable Market): [everyone who COULD use this]
SAM (Serviceable Available Market): [everyone you CAN reach]
SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): [realistic near-term capture]
```

- Use bottom-up calculation, not just top-down. ("10M businesses × $100/month = $1B" is less convincing than "5,000 cafés in Tunis × 200 TND/month = 1M TND/month, scaling to 50,000 businesses nationally")
- Show the wedge: where do you start, how do you expand?

### 6. Business Model (1 slide)

How do you make money?

- Revenue model (subscription, transaction fee, freemium, marketplace)
- Pricing (specific numbers, not "competitive pricing")
- Unit economics (customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, margins)
- Path to profitability (when, at what scale)

### 7. Traction (1-2 slides)

Proof that this isn't just an idea.

Show REAL metrics (whatever you have):
- Users, revenue, growth rate
- Retention/engagement metrics
- Partnerships, letters of intent
- Pilot results, case studies
- Waitlist, pre-orders

Order by impressiveness. Lead with your strongest metric.

**No traction yet?** Show:
- User research and validation
- Prototype testing results
- Market validation (surveys, interviews, competitor analysis)
- Team's domain expertise

### 8. Competition (1 slide)

Acknowledge competition honestly. Show how you're different.

**Not:** "We have no competition" (investors don't believe this)
**Instead:** A positioning matrix showing where you win:

```
               Feature A     Feature B     Price     Local Focus
Competitor 1   Yes           No            High      No
Competitor 2   No            Yes           Medium    No
You            Yes           Yes           Low       Yes ← unfair advantage
```

Or: "Competitors solve X. We solve Y. That matters because Z."

### 9. Team (1 slide)

Why are YOU the people to build this?

- Relevant experience (industry, technical, entrepreneurial)
- Unfair advantages (domain expertise, network, unique insight)
- Key hires needed (shows self-awareness)

### 10. The Ask (1 slide)

What do you need and what will you do with it?

- **Amount:** How much funding/resources/support
- **Use of funds:** Where the money goes (hiring X, building Y, expanding to Z)
- **Milestones:** What you'll achieve with this (specific targets)
- **Timeline:** When you'll hit those milestones

### 11. The Close

End with something memorable:
- Return to the hook (full circle)
- Vision statement (where this goes in 5 years)
- Call to action (what you want the audience to do RIGHT NOW)

## Writing Guidelines

### Storytelling
- Every pitch is a story: **Problem → Tension → Resolution → Vision**
- Use specific examples and real scenarios, not abstractions
- Numbers are more convincing than adjectives ("30% faster" beats "much faster")
- Show, don't tell. A screenshot beats a paragraph. A demo beats a screenshot.

### Slide Design (if applicable)
- One idea per slide. If a slide makes two points, split it.
- More visuals, fewer words. Slides support the speaker — they don't replace them.
- Use consistent visual language (colors, fonts, chart styles)
- Data visualizations should have a clear takeaway (label the insight, not just the data)

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Starting with the solution instead of the problem
- Using jargon the audience doesn't know
- Claiming "no competition" (there's always competition — even "doing nothing" is competition)
- Vanity metrics (downloads without engagement, signups without retention)
- Too many slides (10-15 slides maximum for investor pitches)
- No clear ask (the audience should know exactly what you want)

---

## Hard Rules

1. **NEVER start with the solution.** The audience doesn't care about your product until they care about the problem. Problem first, always.

2. **NEVER use numbers you can't defend.** Every market size claim, growth projection, and metric should have a source or derivation. Made-up numbers destroy credibility instantly.

3. **NEVER hide weaknesses.** Acknowledge them and show how you're addressing them. Investors spot hidden weaknesses anyway — being upfront builds trust.

4. **NEVER pitch features.** Pitch outcomes. "We have real-time analytics" means nothing. "Business owners see which promotions drive repeat visits, increasing retention 2x" is compelling.

5. **NEVER use buzzwords as substance.** "AI-powered," "blockchain-enabled," "disruptive" — these are decorations. Show what the technology DOES, not what it IS.

6. **ALWAYS know your audience.** An investor pitch, a customer pitch, and an internal stakeholder pitch emphasize different things. Tailor the emphasis, not just the content.
